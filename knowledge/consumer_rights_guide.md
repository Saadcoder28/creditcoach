# Consumer Credit Rights and Protections Guide

## Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)
The FCRA is the primary federal law governing credit reporting. Key rights include:

### Your Rights Under FCRA
- You have the right to a free credit report from each bureau once per year at AnnualCreditReport.com
- You must be told if information in your file is used against you (adverse action notice)
- You have the right to dispute inaccurate or incomplete information
- Credit bureaus must investigate disputes within 30 days
- Outdated negative information must be removed (7 years for most items, 10 years for bankruptcy)
- Your credit report cannot be provided to anyone without a permissible purpose
- You can place a security freeze on your credit file for free

### How to Dispute Errors Step by Step
1. Get your free credit report from all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion)
2. Review each report carefully for errors — check every account, balance, and payment status
3. Write a dispute letter identifying each error clearly with specific details
4. Include copies (not originals) of supporting documents such as bank statements or payment receipts
5. Send by certified mail with return receipt requested to each bureau reporting the error
6. The bureau must investigate within 30 days and notify you of results
7. If the item cannot be verified, it must be removed immediately
8. You will receive written results of the investigation and a free updated report
9. If the dispute is not resolved in your favor, you can add a 100-word statement to your file
10. You can also file a complaint with the CFPB at consumerfinance.gov/complaint

### Common Credit Report Errors
- Accounts that do not belong to you (possible identity theft or mixed files)
- Incorrect payment status (showing late when you paid on time)
- Wrong account balances or credit limits
- Accounts listed as open when they are closed
- Duplicate accounts appearing multiple times
- Incorrect personal information (wrong name, address, SSN)
- Outdated negative information that should have been removed after 7 years
- Paid-off debts still showing as outstanding
- Hard inquiries you did not authorize

## Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA)
The ECOA prohibits credit discrimination based on race, color, religion, national origin, sex, marital status, age, or because you receive public assistance.

### Your Rights Under ECOA
- Lenders cannot discourage you from applying for credit based on protected characteristics
- Lenders cannot consider your race, sex, or other protected characteristics in credit decisions
- If denied credit, you have the right to know the specific reasons within 60 days
- You can request the specific reasons for denial in writing
- Lenders must consider reliable public assistance income the same as other income
- You cannot be denied credit solely because you are retired

## Medical Debt Protections
Medical debt has special protections that many consumers do not know about:
- Medical collections under $500 are no longer reported to credit bureaus (as of 2023)
- Paid medical collections are removed from credit reports immediately
- New medical debt has a 12-month waiting period before it can appear on your report
- Veterans Affairs medical debt is excluded from credit reports entirely
- Many hospitals have financial assistance programs — always ask before the debt goes to collections
- Medical debt cannot be reported until after the insurance company has had time to pay
- You can negotiate medical bills — most providers will accept 40-60% of the original amount
- Payment plans do not affect your credit if you stay current on the agreed payments

## Student Loan Protections
- Federal student loans in income-driven repayment plans should show as current on your credit report
- Student loan rehabilitation (9 consecutive on-time payments) removes the default status from your report
- Deferment and forbearance should not count as late payments
- Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF): After 120 qualifying payments while working for a qualifying employer
- Income-driven repayment plans cap payments at 10-20% of discretionary income
- Borrower defense to repayment: If your school defrauded you, you may qualify for loan discharge

## Debt Collection Rights (FDCPA)
The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act protects you from abusive debt collectors:
- Collectors cannot call before 8 AM or after 9 PM in your time zone
- Collectors must stop calling if you send a written cease-and-desist request
- You have the right to request debt validation within 30 days of first contact
- Collectors cannot threaten you with arrest, violence, or use abusive language
- Collectors cannot contact you at work if you tell them your employer disapproves
- Paying a collection does not restart the 7-year reporting clock
- Collectors cannot add unauthorized fees or interest to the debt
- You can negotiate pay-for-delete agreements where the collector removes the account upon payment
- Statute of limitations varies by state (typically 3-6 years) — after this, the debt is time-barred

### How to Handle Collections
1. Request written validation of the debt within 30 days
2. Verify the debt is yours and the amount is correct
3. Check the statute of limitations in your state
4. If valid, negotiate a pay-for-delete agreement in writing before paying
5. Never make a payment without a written agreement
6. If the debt is not yours, dispute it with both the collector and the credit bureaus
7. Keep all correspondence and records of phone calls

## Credit Freeze vs Fraud Alert
### Credit Freeze
- Prevents new accounts from being opened in your name
- Free to place and remove at all three bureaus
- Must be placed with each bureau separately (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion)
- Does not affect your credit score in any way
- You can temporarily lift it when you need to apply for credit
- Recommended for everyone, not just identity theft victims

### Fraud Alert
- Requires lenders to verify your identity before opening new accounts
- Free to place at any one bureau (they must notify the other two)
- Initial fraud alert lasts 1 year
- Extended fraud alert lasts 7 years (requires identity theft report)
- Active duty military can place active duty alerts

## Identity Theft Recovery
If you are a victim of identity theft:
1. File a report at IdentityTheft.gov to create your recovery plan
2. Place a fraud alert or credit freeze at all three bureaus
3. Contact every company where fraud occurred
4. File a police report if needed
5. Review your credit reports for unauthorized accounts
6. Dispute all fraudulent accounts with each credit bureau
7. Consider placing an extended fraud alert (7 years) with an FTC Identity Theft Report

## Where to Get Help
- Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB): consumerfinance.gov
- Federal Trade Commission (FTC): ftc.gov/credit
- National Foundation for Credit Counseling: nfcc.org — free or low-cost credit counseling
- Annual free credit reports: annualcreditreport.com
- CFPB complaint portal: consumerfinance.gov/complaint — file complaints against lenders
- Legal aid: Many states offer free legal services for credit disputes
- HUD-approved housing counselors: Find at hud.gov for mortgage-related credit issues
