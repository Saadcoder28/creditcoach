# Credit Building Strategies for Score Optimization

## Authorized User Tradelines

### How Authorized User Tradelines Work
When someone adds you as an authorized user on their credit card, that card's entire history appears on your credit report. This includes the account's open date, credit limit, payment history, and current balance. You inherit the full timeline of the card, which can instantly add years of positive history to your file.

### Impact on Your Score
- A tradeline with 5+ years of perfect payment history and low utilization can boost a thin file by 30-80 points
- The benefit is greatest when you have few accounts of your own
- Once you have 4-5 of your own tradelines, authorized user accounts have diminishing returns
- FICO 8 (the most commonly used scoring model) gives full weight to authorized user tradelines
- FICO 9 and FICO 10 give reduced weight to authorized user accounts, especially if there is no clear family relationship

### Risks to Consider
- If the primary cardholder misses a payment, the late mark appears on your report too
- If the primary cardholder carries a high balance, that high utilization hits your report
- If you are removed as an authorized user, the tradeline may disappear from your report entirely, which can temporarily lower your score
- Some mortgage lenders will discount or ignore authorized user tradelines during underwriting

### Best Practices
- Only accept authorized user status on cards with perfect payment history and under 10% utilization
- Verify the issuer reports authorized users to all three bureaus (most major issuers do)
- Plan to build your own independent credit within 6-12 months — do not rely on authorized user tradelines long-term

## The All Zeros Strategy

### What It Is
The all zeros strategy means paying off all your credit card balances before your statement closing dates so that every card reports a $0 balance to the bureaus. Some people believe this maximizes their score by showing 0% utilization.

### Why It Can Backfire
- FICO scoring models actually penalize all zeros slightly — reporting $0 on every card can result in a lower score than reporting a small balance on one card
- The scoring model wants to see that you are actively using credit responsibly, not just holding open accounts
- All zeros can signal to the algorithm that you are not using your credit at all

### The Better Approach: The 1% Rule
- Let one card report a small balance — ideally 1% of your total credit limit
- Pay all other cards down to $0 before their statement closing dates
- Example: If you have $10,000 in total credit limits across 3 cards, let one card report a $100 balance and pay the others to zero
- This combination typically produces the highest possible utilization score
- After the statement with the small balance posts, pay it in full by the due date to avoid interest

## Statement Date vs Due Date

### Understanding the Reporting Cycle
Most people confuse these two dates, which leads to accidentally reporting high balances even when they pay in full every month.

### Statement Closing Date
- This is the date your billing cycle ends and your statement is generated
- Your balance on this date is what gets reported to the credit bureaus
- This is the number that determines your utilization percentage
- It is usually 21-25 days before your due date

### Due Date
- This is the deadline for making your payment to avoid late fees and interest
- Paying by the due date keeps your payment history clean
- But if you only pay by the due date, your statement balance (which was already reported) may have been high

### How to Use This to Your Advantage
- Find your statement closing date (check your latest statement or call your issuer)
- Make a payment a few days before the statement closing date to lower the balance that gets reported
- This is called a "pre-statement payment" or "paying before the statement cuts"
- Example: Your closing date is the 15th. You spent $800 on a $1,000 limit card. Pay $750 on the 12th so only $50 is reported (5% utilization instead of 80%)

## Optimal Number of Credit Cards

### What the Data Shows
- FICO high achievers (800+ scores) have an average of 7 credit cards (open and closed combined)
- Having 3-5 open credit cards is generally the sweet spot for score optimization
- More cards means more total available credit, which lowers your overall utilization
- More cards also means more accounts contributing positive payment history

### Risks of Too Many Cards
- Each new application creates a hard inquiry (5-10 point drop)
- New cards lower your average account age, which can temporarily drop your score
- More cards means more accounts to manage and more chances to miss a payment
- Some issuers have rules limiting approvals: Chase's 5/24 rule denies anyone who has opened 5 or more cards with any issuer in the past 24 months

### Risks of Too Few Cards
- With only 1 card, a single high-balance month tanks your utilization
- Your credit mix is thin, which costs you points in the "types of credit" category (10% of FICO)
- You have less total available credit, making utilization harder to manage

### Recommended Approach
- Start with 1 card and add a second after 6-12 months
- Add a third card after another 6-12 months
- Aim for 3-5 cards by the end of year two
- After that, only apply for new cards when you have a specific need or a compelling sign-up bonus
- Never open a card just for the sake of having more cards

## Garden Period Strategy

### What a Garden Period Is
A garden period is a deliberate stretch of time — usually 6 to 12 months — where you stop applying for any new credit. No new credit cards, no loans, no inquiries. You simply tend to your existing accounts like a garden and let them grow.

### Why This Strategy Works
- Hard inquiries from recent applications stop dragging down your score
- Your average account age increases every month without being diluted by new accounts
- Lenders see stability — no recent applications signals that you are not desperate for credit
- Your existing accounts build consistent payment history

### When to Enter a Garden Period
- After opening 2-3 new cards in a short period
- Before applying for a mortgage or auto loan (start gardening at least 6-12 months before)
- After being denied for a card you want — gardening gives your profile time to strengthen
- When your score is stuck in a plateau and you need time-based factors to improve

### What to Do During Your Garden Period
- Pay all bills on time every month
- Keep utilization under 10% across all cards
- Request credit limit increases (soft pull only) every 6 months
- Monitor your credit reports for errors
- Do not close any existing accounts

## Balance Transfer Strategies

### How Balance Transfers Work
A balance transfer moves debt from one credit card to another, usually to take advantage of a lower interest rate. Many cards offer 0% APR on balance transfers for 12-21 months. There is typically a fee of 3-5% of the transferred amount.

### When a Balance Transfer Makes Sense
- You have high-interest credit card debt ($3,000 or more) and a plan to pay it off within the promotional period
- You qualify for a card with a 0% introductory APR
- The balance transfer fee is less than the interest you would pay without transferring
- Example: $5,000 balance at 24% APR = $1,200 in interest per year. A 3% transfer fee = $150. You save $1,050 in the first year alone.

### Impact on Your Credit Score
- Opening a new card creates a hard inquiry and lowers average account age (short-term negative)
- The new card adds available credit, lowering your overall utilization (positive)
- Consolidating balances to one card can spike that card's utilization (negative) — try to keep the transferred balance under 30% of the new card's limit
- Over time, as you pay down the balance, your score should improve significantly

### Common Mistakes
- Transferring a balance and then continuing to spend on the old card, creating more debt
- Not paying off the balance before the promotional period ends — the remaining balance often gets hit with the card's full APR (sometimes retroactively)
- Ignoring the balance transfer fee when calculating savings
- Making a late payment during the promotional period, which can void the 0% APR offer

## Requesting Credit Limit Increases

### Why Credit Limit Increases Matter
A higher credit limit instantly lowers your utilization percentage without requiring you to pay down debt. If you have a $2,000 limit and a $500 balance (25% utilization), getting a limit increase to $5,000 drops your utilization to 10% with zero effort.

### Soft Pull vs Hard Pull
- **Soft pull issuers** (no impact on your score): American Express, Discover, Citi (usually), Capital One (usually via app)
- **Hard pull issuers** (temporarily lowers your score): Chase, Wells Fargo, Bank of America (varies)
- Always ask before requesting: call the number on the back of your card and ask, "Will this require a hard inquiry?" If they say yes, decide whether the limit increase is worth the 5-10 point temporary hit

### Best Time to Request
- After 6 months with the same card and consistent on-time payments
- After a salary increase — issuers factor income into their decisions
- Every 6-12 months for soft pull issuers (there is no downside to asking)
- When your utilization is currently low — issuers are more likely to approve increases when you do not appear to need the money

### Tips for Approval
- Update your income information on your account before requesting
- Have a clean payment history with no missed payments
- Do not request an increase right after opening a new card elsewhere
- Ask for a specific amount — a 25-50% increase over your current limit is a reasonable request

## Debt Avalanche vs Debt Snowball

### Debt Avalanche Method
- List all debts from highest interest rate to lowest
- Make minimum payments on all debts except the one with the highest rate
- Put every extra dollar toward the highest-rate debt first
- When that debt is paid off, move to the next highest rate
- **Example**: Pay off the 24% APR credit card before the 18% APR card before the 6% auto loan
- **Saves the most money** over time because you are eliminating the most expensive debt first

### Debt Snowball Method
- List all debts from smallest balance to largest
- Make minimum payments on all debts except the smallest balance
- Put every extra dollar toward the smallest balance first
- When that debt is paid off, move to the next smallest
- **Example**: Pay off the $400 medical bill before the $2,000 credit card before the $8,000 auto loan
- **Best for motivation** because you see debts disappear quickly, which keeps you going

### Which Is Better for Your Credit Score?
- Both methods improve your score as you pay down debt
- The snowball method may produce faster visible improvements because eliminating an entire account's balance to $0 helps per-account utilization
- The avalanche method saves more money on interest, which frees up cash to pay down other debts faster
- Choose the method that you will actually stick with — the best strategy is the one you follow through on

### A Hybrid Approach
- Use the avalanche method as your default
- But if you have a small debt under $500 that you can eliminate in 1-2 months, knock it out first for the psychological win, then switch to avalanche for the rest

## When to Close vs Keep Open Old Accounts

### When to Keep Accounts Open
- The account has no annual fee — there is no cost to keeping it open
- The account is your oldest credit card — closing it will eventually lower your average account age
- You still have a balance on other cards — closing a card reduces your total available credit, which spikes your utilization
- The account has a long positive payment history that benefits your score

### When Closing an Account May Make Sense
- The card has an annual fee you cannot get waived and you do not use the card's benefits enough to justify the fee
- The card tempts you to spend money you do not have
- You have fraud concerns on that specific account
- You are a victim of a fee-harvester card with excessive charges

### What to Do Instead of Closing
- Call the issuer and ask for a product change (downgrade) to a no-annual-fee card. This preserves your account age and credit limit while eliminating the fee
- If you cannot downgrade, use the card once every 6 months for a small purchase to keep it active. Inactive cards may be closed by the issuer automatically after 12-24 months
- Set a calendar reminder to make a small purchase on each card every 6 months

### Impact of Closing an Account
- Your credit limit on that card goes to zero, which can increase your overall utilization immediately
- The account will continue to appear on your credit report for up to 10 years after closing
- After it falls off your report, your average account age may decrease
- This effect is delayed but can cause a meaningful score drop years later

## Credit Monitoring: Free Options

### Credit Karma
- Free access to your TransUnion and Equifax credit reports
- Uses VantageScore 3.0, which is different from FICO (your number may be 20-40 points different from your FICO score)
- Updates weekly
- Sends alerts when new accounts are opened, inquiries appear, or balances change significantly
- Offers recommendations for credit cards and loans (this is how they make money — take recommendations with a grain of salt)

### Experian Free
- Free access to your Experian credit report and FICO 8 score
- This is the actual FICO score most lenders use, making it more accurate than VantageScore
- Updates monthly (every 30 days)
- Includes Experian Boost to add utility and subscription payments to your report
- Dark web monitoring included in the free tier

### Other Free Monitoring Options
- **Discover Credit Scorecard**: Free FICO score from Experian, available to everyone (you do not need a Discover card)
- **Capital One CreditWise**: Free VantageScore 3.0 from TransUnion, available to everyone (you do not need a Capital One card)
- **Your bank or card issuer**: Many banks provide free FICO scores on your monthly statement or in their app (Chase, Bank of America, Citi, American Express, and others)
- **AnnualCreditReport.com**: Free full credit reports from all three bureaus once per year (now available weekly since 2023)

### What to Monitor For
- New accounts you did not open (possible identity theft)
- Late payments that you believe were paid on time (dispute immediately)
- Sudden drops in your score without explanation
- Balances or credit limits that look wrong
- Hard inquiries you did not authorize
- Collection accounts appearing unexpectedly — you may not have received notice from the collector
