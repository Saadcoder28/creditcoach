# FICO Credit Score: Complete Guide for CreditCoach AI

## What is a FICO Score?
A FICO score is a three-digit number ranging from 300 to 850 that represents your creditworthiness. Lenders use it to decide whether to approve your loan and what interest rate to charge. Higher scores mean lower risk to lenders.

## Score Ranges
- 800-850: Exceptional - Best rates available, easiest approvals
- 740-799: Very Good - Qualifies for most competitive rates
- 670-739: Good - Acceptable to most lenders
- 580-669: Fair - May face higher rates or limited options
- 300-579: Poor - Difficult to get approved, very high rates

## The Five FICO Factors

### 1. Payment History (35% of your score)
This is the single most important factor. It tracks whether you pay your bills on time.

**Impact of late payments:**
- 30 days late: Can drop score 60-110 points depending on starting score
- 60 days late: Can drop score 80-130 points
- 90+ days late: Can drop score 100-150+ points
- The higher your score, the more points you lose from a late payment
- Late payments stay on your report for 7 years but impact decreases over time
- A single late payment after years of on-time payments hurts more than expected

**How to improve:**
- Set up autopay for at least the minimum payment on every account
- Use calendar reminders 5 days before each due date
- If you miss a payment, pay it as soon as possible - 30 days late is better than 60

### 2. Credit Utilization (30% of your score)
This is the percentage of your available credit that you are using. Calculated as: (Total Balances / Total Credit Limits) x 100.

**Utilization thresholds:**
- 0-9%: Excellent - maximum positive impact
- 10-29%: Good - still very favorable
- 30-49%: Fair - starts to hurt your score
- 50-74%: Poor - significant negative impact
- 75-100%: Very Poor - major score damage

**Important details:**
- Both overall utilization AND per-card utilization matter
- Utilization is calculated when your statement closes, not when you pay
- Paying mid-cycle can lower your reported utilization
- Asking for a credit limit increase (without hard pull) instantly lowers utilization

**How to improve:**
- Pay down balances to below 30% of each card limit
- Pay twice a month to keep reported balances low
- Request credit limit increases on existing cards
- Do NOT close old cards - this reduces your total available credit

### 3. Length of Credit History (15% of your score)
This measures how long you have been using credit. It includes the age of your oldest account, newest account, and the average age of all accounts.

**How it works:**
- Older accounts are better
- Opening new accounts lowers your average age
- Closing old accounts can hurt (the account eventually falls off your report)
- Authorized user accounts count toward your history

**How to improve:**
- Keep your oldest accounts open even if you rarely use them
- Use old cards occasionally (small purchase, pay immediately) to keep them active
- Avoid opening many new accounts in a short period
- Consider becoming an authorized user on a family member's old account

### 4. Credit Mix (10% of your score)
FICO rewards having different types of credit accounts, showing you can manage various forms of debt.

**Types of credit:**
- Revolving credit: Credit cards, lines of credit
- Installment loans: Auto loans, student loans, personal loans, mortgages
- Retail accounts: Store credit cards

**How to improve:**
- Having at least one credit card AND one installment loan is ideal
- Do not open accounts you do not need just for mix
- A secured credit card is a good starting point if you have no credit cards

### 5. New Credit Inquiries (10% of your score)
Each time you apply for credit, a hard inquiry appears on your report. Too many inquiries suggest financial distress.

**Impact:**
- Each hard inquiry can lower your score by 5-10 points
- Inquiries affect your score for 12 months and stay on report for 2 years
- Multiple inquiries for the same type of loan within 14-45 days count as one (rate shopping)
- Checking your own credit is a soft inquiry and does NOT affect your score

**How to improve:**
- Only apply for credit when you truly need it
- When shopping for a mortgage or auto loan, do all applications within a 2-week window
- Use pre-qualification tools that do soft pulls instead of hard pulls

## Common Scenarios and Score Impact Estimates

### Positive Actions
- Making 6 months of on-time payments: +20 to +40 points
- Paying credit card balance from 80% to 20% utilization: +30 to +60 points
- Hard inquiry falling off after 12 months: +5 to +10 points
- Collection account being removed: +25 to +75 points
- Becoming authorized user on old account with perfect history: +15 to +30 points

### Negative Actions
- One 30-day late payment: -60 to -110 points
- One 60-day late payment: -80 to -130 points
- Maxing out a credit card: -20 to -45 points
- Account going to collections: -75 to -125 points
- Applying for 5+ credit cards in a month: -25 to -50 points
- Closing your oldest credit card: -10 to -30 points
- Filing for bankruptcy: -130 to -240 points

## Building Credit from Scratch
If you have no credit history (credit invisible):
1. Get a secured credit card (requires a deposit, typically $200-500)
2. Become an authorized user on a family member's card
3. Look into credit-builder loans from credit unions
4. Report rent payments through services like Experian Boost
5. Use the secured card for small purchases, pay in full each month
6. After 6 months of responsible use, you should have a scoreable file

## Timeline for Credit Improvement
- 1 month: Utilization changes reflected immediately
- 3 months: New on-time payment pattern begins to show
- 6 months: Significant improvement possible if utilization lowered and payments on time
- 12 months: Hard inquiries stop affecting score, consistent history established
- 24 months: Late payments from 2 years ago have reduced impact
- 7 years: Most negative items fall off your credit report
