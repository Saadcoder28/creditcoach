# Credit Dispute Templates and Process Guide

## Step-by-Step Dispute Process With the Three Bureaus

### Before You File a Dispute
1. Get your free credit reports from all three bureaus at AnnualCreditReport.com
2. Review each report line by line — check every account, balance, payment status, and personal detail
3. Identify every error and gather supporting documentation (bank statements, payment confirmations, correspondence with creditors)
4. Decide whether to dispute online or by mail (see comparison below)

### Filing the Dispute
1. Write a separate dispute letter or submit a separate online dispute for each error on each bureau's report
2. Clearly identify the account (include account number, creditor name, and the specific information that is wrong)
3. State what the correct information should be
4. Attach copies of supporting documents — never send originals
5. If mailing, send by certified mail with return receipt requested so you have proof of delivery

### Bureau Contact Information
- **Equifax**: P.O. Box 740256, Atlanta, GA 30374 | Online: equifax.com/personal/disputes
- **Experian**: P.O. Box 4500, Allen, TX 75013 | Online: experian.com/disputes
- **TransUnion**: P.O. Box 2000, Chester, PA 19016 | Online: transunion.com/disputes

### After You File
- The bureau must investigate within 30 days (45 days if you provide additional information during the investigation)
- The bureau contacts the creditor (called the "data furnisher") and asks them to verify the information
- If the creditor cannot verify the information, it must be removed from your report
- You will receive written results of the investigation and a free updated credit report
- If the dispute is resolved in your favor, the bureau must notify any creditor who received your report in the last 6 months

## How to Dispute Online vs By Mail

### Online Disputes
**Pros:**
- Faster submission — results sometimes come back in 7-14 days
- Easy to track status through the bureau's website
- Upload supporting documents directly

**Cons:**
- You may be limited in how much detail you can provide (character limits on explanation fields)
- The online system may try to categorize your dispute, which can result in a generic investigation
- You lose the paper trail that certified mail provides
- Some consumer attorneys recommend against online disputes because you may unknowingly agree to terms that limit your rights (such as binding arbitration)

### Mail Disputes
**Pros:**
- You can provide unlimited detail in your letter
- Certified mail with return receipt creates a legal paper trail
- You retain full rights under the FCRA
- Better for complex disputes or disputes you may need to escalate

**Cons:**
- Slower — budget 30-45 days for the full process
- Requires printing, mailing, and tracking
- Supporting documents must be photocopied and included

### Recommendation
- For simple errors (wrong address, duplicate account): online is fine
- For serious errors (inaccurate late payments, accounts that are not yours, identity theft): use certified mail
- For any dispute you might escalate to the CFPB or an attorney: always use certified mail

## What to Include in Every Dispute

### Required Information
- Your full legal name, current address, date of birth, and last four digits of your Social Security number
- The specific account you are disputing (creditor name, account number)
- A clear explanation of what is wrong and what the correct information should be
- The action you want taken (remove the item, update the payment status, correct the balance, etc.)

### Supporting Documents to Attach (Copies Only)
- Bank statements showing payments were made on time
- Canceled checks or payment confirmation receipts
- Letters from the creditor acknowledging the error
- Identity theft reports from IdentityTheft.gov (if applicable)
- Court documents (for bankruptcies, judgments, or liens that have been resolved)
- A copy of your credit report with the disputed items highlighted or circled

## Sample Dispute Letter for Inaccurate Late Payments

### Template

[Your Full Name]
[Your Address]
[City, State, ZIP]
[Date]

[Bureau Name]
[Bureau Dispute Address]

Re: Dispute of Inaccurate Late Payment Reporting
Account: [Creditor Name], Account Number: [XXXX-XXXX-XXXX-1234]

To Whom It May Concern:

I am writing to dispute inaccurate information on my credit report. Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act, Section 611, I have the right to dispute any information I believe to be inaccurate.

The following account is reporting a late payment that is incorrect:

Creditor: [Creditor Name]
Account Number: [Last 4 digits or full number]
Reported Late Payment Date: [Month, Year]
Reported Status: [e.g., "30 days late"]

This information is inaccurate. I made my payment on [date] via [method — online banking, check, auto-pay, etc.]. I have enclosed [describe documents — bank statement, payment confirmation, etc.] as evidence that this payment was made on time.

I request that you investigate this matter and correct the payment status to "Paid as Agreed" for the month in question. Please send me written confirmation of the results of your investigation and an updated copy of my credit report.

Sincerely,
[Your Signature]
[Your Printed Name]

Enclosures: [List of attached documents]

## Sample Goodwill Letter

### What a Goodwill Letter Is
A goodwill letter is sent directly to the creditor (not the bureau) asking them to remove an accurate late payment from your credit report as a gesture of goodwill. This works best when you have a long history of on-time payments and the late payment was due to an unusual circumstance (job loss, medical emergency, simple oversight).

### Template

[Your Full Name]
[Your Address]
[City, State, ZIP]
[Date]

[Creditor Name]
[Creditor Address]

Re: Goodwill Adjustment Request
Account Number: [XXXX-XXXX-XXXX-1234]

Dear [Creditor Name] Customer Service,

I am writing to request a goodwill adjustment on my account referenced above. I have been a loyal customer of [Creditor Name] since [year], and I value our relationship.

In [month, year], my payment was reported as [30/60/90] days late. I want to be transparent about what happened: [Provide a brief, honest explanation. Examples: "I was hospitalized and unable to manage my bills during that period," or "I experienced an unexpected job loss that temporarily impacted my ability to stay current," or "I made an oversight and missed my payment deadline by a few days."]

Since that time, I have made every payment on time and have maintained my account in good standing. I have been a customer for [X] years and this is the only blemish on my payment history with your company.

I understand that the late payment was reported accurately, and I am not disputing the information. Instead, I am respectfully asking if you would consider removing this late payment from my credit report as a goodwill gesture. This negative mark is significantly impacting my ability to [explain your situation — qualify for a mortgage, refinance a loan, etc.].

I would greatly appreciate your consideration. Thank you for your time.

Sincerely,
[Your Signature]
[Your Printed Name]

### Tips for Goodwill Letters
- Be polite and take responsibility — never be confrontational or threatening
- Keep it to one page
- Send it to the executive office or CEO address, not the general customer service address (search online for "[creditor name] executive office address")
- Follow up with a phone call 2-3 weeks after sending
- Success rates vary — some creditors have policies against goodwill adjustments, but many will help loyal customers. Capital One and Discover are historically more receptive

## Sample Debt Validation Letter

### What Debt Validation Is
Under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA), you have the right to request that a debt collector prove you actually owe the debt. You must send this letter within 30 days of their first contact with you. Once you send it, the collector must stop collection activity until they provide validation.

### Template

[Your Full Name]
[Your Address]
[City, State, ZIP]
[Date]

[Collection Agency Name]
[Collection Agency Address]

Re: Debt Validation Request
Reference Number / Account Number: [Number from their letter]

To Whom It May Concern:

I received your correspondence dated [date] regarding an alleged debt. I am writing to exercise my rights under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act, Section 809(b), to request validation of this debt.

Please provide the following information:

1. The name and address of the original creditor
2. The original account number with the original creditor
3. The amount of the original debt and an itemized accounting of all fees, interest, and charges that make up the current balance
4. Proof that you are licensed to collect debts in [your state]
5. A copy of the original signed agreement or contract between me and the original creditor
6. Documentation showing the chain of ownership if this debt has been sold or assigned

Until you provide this validation, I request that you cease all collection activity on this account and refrain from reporting this debt to any credit reporting agency.

Please note that this is not a refusal to pay. This is a request for verification that this debt is valid, that the amount is correct, and that you have the legal authority to collect it.

Sincerely,
[Your Signature]
[Your Printed Name]

Send via certified mail, return receipt requested.

### Important Notes
- You must send this within 30 days of receiving the collector's initial notice
- If they cannot validate the debt, they must stop contacting you and remove it from your credit report
- Many old debts change hands multiple times, and collectors often cannot produce original documentation
- Even if the debt is valid, collectors frequently report the wrong amount — validation catches this

## Sample Pay-for-Delete Negotiation Letter

### What Pay-for-Delete Is
Pay-for-delete is a negotiation where you offer to pay a collection account in exchange for the collector agreeing to remove the account from your credit reports entirely. This is not guaranteed — collectors are not required to agree, and some refuse as a matter of policy. But many smaller collection agencies will agree, especially on older debts.

### Template

[Your Full Name]
[Your Address]
[City, State, ZIP]
[Date]

[Collection Agency Name]
[Collection Agency Address]

Re: Settlement and Deletion Offer
Reference Number / Account Number: [Number]
Original Creditor: [Name]
Reported Balance: [Amount]

To Whom It May Concern:

I am writing to propose a resolution for the account referenced above. I am prepared to make a payment of $[your offer — typically 40-60% of the reported balance] as payment in full to settle this account, contingent on the following condition:

Upon receipt and clearance of my payment, [Collection Agency Name] agrees to request deletion of this account from my credit reports with all three credit reporting agencies (Equifax, Experian, and TransUnion) within 30 days of payment.

If this arrangement is acceptable, please respond in writing on your company letterhead with the following confirmed:
1. The agreed settlement amount
2. Confirmation that the account will be reported as "deleted" (not "paid in full" or "settled") to all three credit bureaus
3. Confirmation that no further collection activity will occur on this account

I will submit payment within 10 business days of receiving your written agreement. Payment will be made by [cashier's check or money order — do not give them your bank account information].

Please respond to this letter at the address above. This offer is valid for 30 days from the date of this letter.

Sincerely,
[Your Signature]
[Your Printed Name]

### Tips for Pay-for-Delete Negotiations
- Start by offering 30-40% of the balance — many collectors purchased the debt for pennies on the dollar
- Never pay until you have the agreement in writing on their letterhead
- Pay with a cashier's check or money order — never give bank account or debit card information to a collector
- Keep all correspondence and proof of payment indefinitely
- If they agree verbally but refuse to put it in writing, do not pay — verbal agreements are unenforceable
- Smaller collection agencies are more likely to agree than large national agencies
- Debts over 3 years old are easier to negotiate because collectors know the statute of limitations may be approaching

## Timeline: The 30-Day Investigation Window

### What Happens After You Dispute
- **Day 1**: The bureau receives your dispute (online disputes are received immediately; mailed disputes arrive in 3-7 days)
- **Days 1-5**: The bureau reviews your dispute and sends a notice to the creditor or data furnisher
- **Days 5-25**: The creditor investigates and either verifies, updates, or fails to respond
- **Day 30**: The bureau must complete its investigation (45 days if you provided additional information during the process)
- **Days 30-35**: You receive written results and, if the dispute was successful, an updated credit report

### If the Creditor Does Not Respond
- If the creditor fails to respond to the bureau's inquiry within the investigation period, the disputed information must be removed
- This is one of the most common ways disputes succeed — creditors handle thousands of verification requests and sometimes fail to respond in time

### If Your Dispute Is Denied
- You have the right to add a 100-word consumer statement to your credit report explaining your side
- You can re-dispute if you have new evidence or information
- You can escalate to the CFPB
- You can consult with a consumer credit attorney (many offer free consultations)

## Escalation: CFPB Complaint Process

### When to File a CFPB Complaint
- Your dispute was denied by the bureau and you believe the decision is wrong
- The bureau did not complete its investigation within 30 days
- A creditor or collector violated your rights under the FCRA or FDCPA
- You are getting nowhere with the bureau's standard dispute process

### How to File
1. Go to consumerfinance.gov/complaint
2. Select the category (credit reporting, debt collection, etc.)
3. Provide detailed information about your complaint, including dates, account numbers, and what happened
4. Attach any supporting documents
5. Submit the complaint — you will receive a tracking number

### What Happens Next
- The CFPB forwards your complaint to the company within 1 business day
- The company has 15 days to acknowledge receipt and 60 days to provide a final response
- The company's response is posted to the CFPB complaint database (publicly available)
- Companies take CFPB complaints seriously because the data is tracked and can lead to regulatory action
- CFPB complaints have a significantly higher resolution rate than standard disputes — many issues that were denied through normal channels get resolved after a CFPB complaint

### Tips for Effective CFPB Complaints
- Be specific and factual — include dates, names, account numbers, and a clear timeline
- Explain what you have already tried (direct disputes, phone calls, etc.)
- State the outcome you want (removal of inaccurate information, cease collection activity, etc.)
- Attach all relevant documents
- Keep the tone professional — the CFPB reviews complaints from both sides

## Common Dispute Reasons: What Works vs What Does Not

### Reasons That Tend to Work
- **Account does not belong to you**: Mixed credit files (where another person's account appears on your report) are more common than people realize, especially if you have a common name. Providing your ID and proof of address usually resolves this
- **Incorrect payment status**: If you have proof you paid on time (bank statement, confirmation email), bureaus will correct this
- **Wrong balance or credit limit reported**: Providing a recent statement showing the correct numbers is straightforward evidence
- **Outdated information**: Negative items that are past the 7-year reporting window must be removed. Calculate the date from the first delinquency, not the date the account was closed or sent to collections
- **Duplicate accounts**: The same debt appearing twice inflates negative information and must be corrected
- **Identity theft accounts**: With a police report and FTC identity theft affidavit, fraudulent accounts can be removed

### Reasons That Typically Do Not Work
- **"This is not mine" when it actually is yours**: Falsely disputing accurate information can backfire. Creditors will verify it, and repeated frivolous disputes can lead the bureau to dismiss future disputes
- **"I don't remember this debt"**: Not remembering is not the same as the information being inaccurate
- **Disputing accurate late payments without goodwill context**: If you were genuinely late and the creditor has records, the dispute will be denied. Use a goodwill letter instead
- **Blanket disputes on every item**: Disputing everything at once looks like a dispute mill tactic. Bureaus can classify these as frivolous and refuse to investigate
- **"Credit repair company told me to dispute this"**: Bureaus flag patterns associated with credit repair companies and may reject the dispute as frivolous

### Strategic Approach to Disputes
1. Only dispute information you genuinely believe is inaccurate or unverifiable
2. Dispute one or two items at a time — targeted disputes get better results
3. Include specific evidence for each disputed item
4. If a dispute fails, try the goodwill letter route with the creditor directly
5. Escalate to the CFPB only after exhausting direct dispute options
6. Keep records of everything — dates, tracking numbers, responses, phone calls
